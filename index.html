💻 Lesson 1 — The Information Age

What is the Information Age?

The Information Age (also: Digital Age or New Media Age) is the period beginning in the last quarter of the 20th century when information became effortlessly accessible and widely available through computer technology. Digital information is the key driver across all sectors.

Origin: Started from the invention of the personal computer, amplified by the arrival of the Internet. IT has grown exponentially since then.

Why "Digital Age"?

Because digital information — data in binary (0s and 1s) — drives commerce, education, healthcare, government, entertainment. Everything analog has been digitized.

Why "New Media Age"?

New interactive media (internet, social media, streaming) replaced or supplemented old media (print, broadcast TV). New media is interactive, personalized, participatory — users create, not just consume.

Core idea: The Information Age represents a fundamental shift in how civilization organizes, accesses, and uses knowledge. Whoever controls information holds significant power.

Mass Media — In Depth

Mass Media = technology for the transfer of information and ideas to the general audience. Targets large, heterogeneous audiences.

Traditional:
• Television — visual broadcast
• Radio — audio broadcast
• Newspapers — print journalism
• Magazines — periodic publications
• Books — long-form print
New/Digital:
• Computers and software
• Internet and websites
• Social media platforms
• Streaming services
• Podcasts and digital radio

Functions of Mass Media

  • Inform — news and current events to general public
  • Educate — documentaries, instructional, e-learning
  • Entertain — films, music, sports
  • Persuade — advertising, political campaigns, PSAs
  • Connect — social media enabling mass personal communication
  • Preserve culture — recording history, traditions, art
In the present era, internet connectivity has become a basic commodity. People depend on it for education, work, entertainment, business, research, and socialization.

Computers — Definition, 7 Types & Bioinformatics

A computer = electronic device that can store, retrieve, and process data. All three functions define a computer.

7 Types of Computers

1. Personal Computer (PC)
General-purpose for individual use. Includes Windows and Mac.
2. Desktop Computer
Stationary PC with separate components (monitor, keyboard, tower).
3. Laptop
Portable integrated computer. First made in 1983.
4. PDA
Personal Digital Assistant — handheld organizer. Predecessor to smartphones.
5. Server
Provides data, resources, or services to other computers over a network.
6. Mainframe
Large, powerful computer for bulk data processing (banking, census).
7. Wearable Computer — Computing devices worn on the body: smartwatches, fitness trackers, AR glasses.

Bioinformatics

The human brain cannot store all genetic sequences of organisms. Bioinformatics = application of IT to store, organize, and analyze vast amounts of biological data.

The Internet & WWW

Internet = worldwide system of interconnected networks that facilitate data transmission among innumerable computers.

  • Originally used by scientists to communicate with other scientists
  • Remained under government control until 1984
  • Early problem: speed — solved by fiber-optic cables (billions of bits/minute)
  • Email = a suitable way to send messages digitally
  • Today, internet = a basic commodity
Internet: The physical infrastructure — cables, servers, routers, protocols connecting billions of devices worldwide.
World Wide Web (WWW): System of interlinked hypertext documents accessed via the internet. Invented by Tim Berners-Lee (1989). Internet existed before WWW.

Information Ethics — P.A.P.A.

🔒 PRIVACY
Right to control what personal info is collected, how it's used, who accesses it.
Issues: Data breaches, surveillance, tracking, facial recognition.
✅ ACCURACY

🌿 Lesson 2 — Biodiversity & the Healthy Society

What is Biodiversity?

Biodiversity = vast variety of life forms in the entire Earth, defined in the structural and functional perspective — not as individual species. It is the total variety of life on Earth and how all living things fit together.

Biodiversity acts as a safety net: the more diverse an ecosystem, the more resilient it is to changes like climate change or disease.

Ecosystem Services

  • Provisioning: Food, water, fiber, fuel, genetic resources
  • Regulating: Climate regulation, flood control, water purification, disease regulation
  • Cultural: Recreation, ecotourism, spiritual value, aesthetic value
  • Supporting: Nutrient cycling, soil formation, photosynthesis
Key module quote: "Nature is our infrastructure. When we fail to care for it, we are not just losing a scenery; we are dismantling the systems that regulate our climate, filter our water, and protect us from disease."

Significant decline in biodiversity has direct human impact when ecosystems can no longer provide the physical as well as social needs of human beings.

Three Types of Biodiversity

1. ECOSYSTEM DIVERSITY

Variety of habitats, biological communities, and ecological processes.

Examples: Tropical rainforests, coral reefs, grasslands, wetlands, deserts, tundra.

Why it matters: Loss of one ecosystem type = loss of unique species and services.
2. SPECIES DIVERSITY

Number and relative abundance of different species within an area.

Example: A coral reef with 500 fish species has higher diversity than a monoculture farm.

Why it matters: More species = more stability and resilience.
3. GENETIC DIVERSITY

Total variation in genetic characteristics within a species/population.

Examples: Rice varieties, dog breeds, wild wolf coat color differences.

Why it matters: Allows populations to adapt. Low diversity = vulnerability to disease.
Hierarchy: Genetic diversity → Species diversity → Ecosystem diversity. All three are interconnected.

5 Threats to Biodiversity — In Detail

1. Habitat Loss and Destruction

Natural habitat becomes unable to support species present; organisms displaced or destroyed. Major cause: human inhabitation and economic land use (deforestation, urban sprawl, agricultural expansion).

2. Alteration in Ecosystem Composition

Changes caused by introduction of invasive species or removal of keystone species. Invasive species outcompete and kill off local species, flipping the entire balance of the area.

3. Over-exploitation

Harvesting renewable resources to diminishing returns — taking individuals faster than natural reproduction. Module example: overfishing bluefin tuna — not given time to breed.

4. Pollution and Contamination

Harmful materials (pollutants) cause adverse ecosystem changes. Chemicals, plastics, waste make air/water/soil toxic. Not just kills directly — also causes reproductive issues and disease. Examples: sea turtles eating plastic bags; pesticides killing soil microbes.

5. Global Climate Change

Long-term temperature and weather shifts — species cannot adapt fast enough. Examples: polar bears losing ice; coral bleaching from rising ocean temperatures. 33% of reef-forming corals, sharks and shark relatives face extinction.

Health, Biology & Biodiversity

The interrelation between human health and biological diversity is considerable and complex. Basic needs (air, water, food, habitat) are provided by ecosystems.

Environmental hazards increase the risk of cancer, heart disease, asthma, and many other illnesses.

Managing Benefits and Risks

  • Pesticides → increase crop yields BUT pose health/environment hazards
  • Energy production → sustains life BUT creates pollution hazards
  • Fossil fuel taxes → lower emissions BUT increase transportation costs
Social justice: People with lower socio-economic status have greater exposure to harmful environmental conditions.

Bats as Pollinators

In warm climates, bats are important pollinators and seed dispersers of many plants important to humans. The ongoing over-exploitation of pollinating insects and animals threatens global crop production.

🧬 Lesson 3 — Genetically Modified Organisms

What is a GMO?

GMO (Genetically Modified Organism) = organism created through genetic engineering in which the genetic material (DNA) has been altered in a way that does not occur naturally by mating or natural recombination.

Genetic Engineering Process on a Plant (5 Steps)

  1. DNA Extraction and Isolation — desired gene extracted from donor organism
  2. Cloning and Designing Genes — gene replicated and modified
  3. Transformation — gene inserted into target plant cells
  4. Cell and Tissue Culture — transformed cells grown in culture
  5. Plant Breeding — successfully transformed plants grown and selected
Why develop GMOs? The development of GMOs was perceived to help in the advancement of technology for the benefit of humans in different industries — to solve food security, disease, and environmental problems.

Key fact: Human insulin — used by millions of diabetics — is produced by GMO bacteria (E. coli with human insulin gene inserted). This replaced extraction from pig pancreases.

7 Benefits of GMOs

1. Higher Farming Efficiency
Less herbicides/pesticides; lower labor and cultivation costs.
2. Increased Harvest
Pest/disease resistance = less crop loss, more yield per hectare.
3. Control in Fertility
Controlling hybrid seed purity ensures higher, predictable yields.
4. Improved Food Processing
Altered characteristics ease industrial food processing.
5. Improved Desirable Characteristics
Longer shelf life, enhanced color/taste, modified enzyme production.
6. Nutritional Enhancement
Golden Rice (Vit A & iron), maize with lysine. Edible vaccines for viral and diarrheal diseases.
7. Reduced Fertilizer & Pesticide Use — decreasing environmental pollution and health exposure for farmers and consumers.

Potential Risks of GMOs

Scientific Concerns

  • Genetic engineering is a young science — inadequate long-term studies
  • Promotes mutation in organisms whose long-term effects are unknown

Human Health Risks

Allergic Reactions
Transferred genes may introduce allergenic proteins. Brazil nut gene in soy caused allergic reactions — discontinued.
Gene Mutation
GMO DNA fragments could transfer to gut bacteria or body cells, causing unintended changes.
Antibiotic Resistance
Antibiotic resistance marker genes could transfer to gut microbes.
Nutritional Value Changes
Engineering may inadvertently alter nutritional composition.

Other Risks

  • May alter microorganism balance in human digestive system
  • Production of toxins detrimental to health
  • Production of allergens
  • Human Genome Project concerns
  • Mutation of genetically engineered microorganisms
  • Cloning — ethical and safety concerns

⚛️ Lesson 4 — The Nano World

Nanoscience & Nanotechnology

NANOSCIENCE
Discipline of understanding extremely small things using accepted experimental and theoretical techniques.
NANOTECHNOLOGY
The manipulation of matter on an atomic or subatomic scale.

How Small is a Nanometer?

  • 1 nm = one billionth of a meter (10⁻⁹ m)
  • Piece of paper ≈ 100,000 nm thick
  • Human hair cross-section ≈ 80,000–100,000 nm
  • DNA strand ≈ 2.5 nm diameter
  • Gold atom ≈ 0.3 nm diameter
  • Red blood cell ≈ 7,000 nm wide | Pinhead ≈ 1,000,000 nm wide
Module analogy: "If a nanoparticle is as big as the size of the soccer ball, the soccer ball would then be as big as the earth."

Why Work at Nanoscale?

At nanoscale, materials exhibit unique quantum properties — gold nanoparticles appear red/purple; carbon nanotubes are 100x stronger than steel at 1/6 the weight. Surface area massively increases reactivity.

3 Microscopes for Viewing Nanomaterials

1. Electron Microscope

Utilizes a particle beam of electrons to light up a specimen and develop a well-magnified image.

  • Resolution: ~0.1 nm (can see individual atoms)
  • TEM — electrons pass through specimen; shows internal structure
  • SEM — electrons scan surface; shows 3D surface detail
  • Limitation: Cannot image living samples; requires vacuum

2. Atomic Force Microscope (AFM)

Makes use of a mechanical probe that gathers information from the surface of a material.

  • Cantilever with ultra-sharp tip scans surface
  • Works in air, liquid, or vacuum
  • Can image non-conductive materials including biological samples
  • Can measure forces between molecules

3. Scanning Tunneling Microscope (STM)

Enables scientists to VIEW AND MANIPULATE nanoscale particles, atoms, and small molecules.

  • First to image individual atoms (IBM, 1981)
  • Uses electron tunneling phenomenon
  • Can push, pull, and position individual atoms
  • Requires conductive surfaces and high vacuum

Nanomanufacturing — 2 Fundamental Approaches

Nanomanufacturing = scaled-up, reliable, and cost-effective manufacturing of nanoscale materials, structures, devices, and systems. Leads to new products and improved materials.

⬆️ BOTTOM-UP FABRICATION
Builds products from atomic- and molecular-scale components upward.
Analogy: Building a house brick by brick.

Methods:
• Sol-gel processing
• Chemical vapor deposition
• Plasma or flame spraying
• Laser pyrolysis
• Microemulsion
⬇️ TOP-DOWN FABRICATION
Trims down large pieces of materials to nanoscale.
Analogy: Sculpting a statue from marble.

Methods:
• Lithography
• Laser ablation
• Sputtering deposition
• Pulsed electrochemical etching
• Vapor deposition

7 New Approaches to Nanomaterial Assembly

MethodHow It Works
Dip Pen LithographyAFM tip "dipped" in chemical fluid, then used to "write" on a surface — like a nanoscale fountain pen. ~15 nm resolution.
Self-AssemblyComponents join to form organized structure WITHOUT outside direction — driven by physical/chemical forces. Inspired by how DNA and proteins self-assemble in nature.
Chemical Vapor DepositionChemicals react to form very pure, high-performance films on a substrate. Used for semiconductors and carbon nanotubes.
Nanoimprint LithographyGenerating nanoscale attributes by "stamping" or "printing" onto a surface using a mold — like a nanoscale rubber stamp.
Molecular Beam EpitaxyDepositing extremely controlled thin films atom by atom in ultra-high vacuum. Used for precision semiconductor devices.
Roll-to-Roll ProcessingConstructing nanoscale devices on a roll of ultrathin plastic or metal. Enables large-scale, low-cost flexible electronics.
Atomic Layer EpitaxyLaying down one-atom-thick layers on a surface. Extreme precision for atomic-level thickness control.

3 Distinct Features of the Nanoscale

1. Scale Where Biology Occurs

Most biological processes — protein folding, DNA replication, enzyme-substrate interactions — happen at 1–100 nm. Understanding nanoscale = understanding life itself.
2. Quantum Effects Dominate

At nanoscale, quantum mechanical effects become significant. Electrons behave as waves. Materials gain unique electrical, optical, and magnetic properties (quantum confinement).
3. Far Larger Surface Areas

Nanoscale materials have far larger surface areas than similar masses of larger-scale materials. Massively increases reactivity. 1g nanoparticles >> 1g bulk material surface area.

Benefits and Concerns by Sector

General concerns: not a single technology; may become pervasive; difficult to detect without specialist tools; may make current practices obsolete.

🧪 Lesson 5 — The Aspects of Gene Therapy

Gene Therapy — Definition and 3 Approaches

Gene Therapy = medical technique using genes to treat or prevent diseases by replacing defective genes with healthy ones.

3 Approaches (Fliesler, 2017)

1. REPLACE
Replacement of a mutated gene that causes disease with a healthy copy of the gene.
Example: Replacing defective CFTR gene causing cystic fibrosis.
2. INACTIVATE
Inactivation of a mutated gene that is functioning improperly.
Example: Silencing an overactive cancer-promoting gene via RNA interference.
3. INTRODUCE
Introducing a new gene into the body to help fight a disease.
Example: Adding tumor suppressor genes to cancer cells.
Why a carrier is required: A gene cannot be directly inserted into a human gene or cell — it must be delivered using a carrier or vector.

Vectors — Gene Delivery Systems

Genes need a carrier (vector) to enter the cell nucleus. The module diagram shows an adenovector delivering the correct gene.

Viral Vectors (most common)
Modified viruses with harmful genes removed, replaced by therapeutic gene.
• Adenoviruses (adenovector) — shown in module diagram
• Retroviruses — integrate into host genome
• Lentiviruses — infect non-dividing cells
• AAV (Adeno-associated virus) — most used in clinical trials
Non-Viral Vectors
Alternative delivery:
• Liposomes — fat-based nanoparticles
• Naked DNA injection
• Gene gun — DNA on gold particles
• Electroporation — electric pulses open cell pores
Sickle cell disease gene therapy (from module diagram):
Step 1: Collection of blood-forming stem cells from patient
Step 2: Stem cells corrected by beta-globin gene transfer via viral vectors
Step 3: Transplantation of corrected stem cells back to patient

Two Types of Gene Therapy

SOMATIC GENE THERAPY

Manipulation of genes in somatic (body) cells.

✅ Benefits the patient only
❌ NOT inherited by next generation

Targets: Blood, lung, liver, muscle cells.
Status: Most current clinical trials. More ethically acceptable.
Example: CAR-T cell therapy for leukemia.
GERM-LINE GENE THERAPY

Modification of germ cells (eggs, sperm, early embryos).

✅ Changes ARE inherited by children and future generations
⚠️ Permanent, heritable modification

Targets: Eggs, sperm, zygotes, early embryos. ALL cells affected.
Status: Banned/restricted in most countries.
Controversial example: 2018 "CRISPR babies" in China.
KEY EXAM DISTINCTION: Somatic = affects only the patient (NOT inherited). Germ-line = affects all future generations (IS inherited).

Stem Cells — Complete Overview

Stem cells = mother cells that have the potential to become any type of cell in the body.

Key Properties

  • Can become ANY cell type — blood, heart, bones, skin, muscles, brain
  • Self-renewal — multiply while maintaining developmental potential
  • Differentiation — become specialized cells

Two Types by Source

EMBRYONIC STEM CELLS (ESCs)

Derived from a 4–5 day old human embryo in the blastocyst phase.

Properties: Totipotent — can become ANY cell type (highest potential).
Controversy: Extracting ESCs destroys the embryo — major ethical debate.
SOMATIC (ADULT) STEM CELLS

Exist throughout the body after embryonic development, found in different tissue types.

Properties: Multipotent — several related cell types.
Examples: Hematopoietic stem cells (bone marrow → blood), neural stem cells.

🌡️ Lesson 6 — Climate Change

What is Climate Change?

  • Statistically significant changes in climate for a continuous period of time
  • A long-term shift of Earth's average weather patterns
  • Frequently used interchangeably with global warming — a continuing temperature increase due to human activities
  • Primarily driven by Industrial Revolution's reliance on fossil fuels — accelerating the natural greenhouse effect at a rate unprecedented in geological history
Climate vs. Weather: Weather = today's conditions. Climate = 30+ year average patterns. Climate change = long-term shifts, not individual events.

Natural Causes of Climate Change

🌋 Volcanic Eruptions

Volcanoes emit: CO₂, SO₂, salt crystals, volcanic ash/dust, bacteria, viruses

Cooling Effect: Sulfate aerosols reach the stratosphere and act as a reflective shield, blocking a percentage of incoming solar radiation → temporary cooling of Earth's surface.

Near EQUATOR: MORE likely to cause global cooling — wind patterns distribute aerosols globally.
Near POLES: LESS likely to cause global cooling — polar winds keep aerosols in limited area.
Example: Mount Pinatubo (1991, Philippines) cooled global temps by ~0.5°C for 1–2 years.

🌍 Orbital Changes — Milankovitch Theory

Earth's orbit can also cause climate change. Cyclical variations in three elements of Earth-Sun geometry produce variations in solar energy reaching Earth:

ECCENTRICITY
Shape of Earth's orbit (circular → elliptical).

Cycle: ~100,000 years

More elliptical = greater seasonal variation in solar energy.
OBLIQUITY
Variation of Earth's axial tilt (22.1°–24.5°).

Cycle: ~41,000 years

Greater tilt = more extreme seasons.
PRECESSION
Wobble of Earth's rotational axis orientation.

Cycle: ~26,000 years

Changes which hemisphere receives most intense sunshine.
Important: Milankovitch cycles explain ice ages over geological timescales (thousands of years). They CANNOT explain the rapid warming of the past 150 years — that is human-caused.

9 Pieces of Evidence of Climate Change

🌡️ 1. Global Temperature Rise
~1.1°C increase since pre-industrial times.
🌊 2. Warming Ocean
Oceans absorbed 90%+ of excess heat from enhanced greenhouse effect.
🏔️ 3. Shrinking Ice Sheets
Greenland and Antarctic ice sheets losing mass at accelerating rates.
🧊 4. Glacial Retreat
Glaciers worldwide retreating; many lost 30–50% of mass since 1900.
❄️ 5. Decreased Snow Cover
Northern Hemisphere spring snow cover decreasing since 1980s.
📈 6. Rise of Sea Level
~20 cm rise since 1900; rate accelerating.
🧊 7. Declining Arctic Sea Ice
Arctic summer sea ice declining ~13% per decade.
⚡ 8. Extreme Events
Increasing frequency/intensity of heat waves, droughts, floods, typhoons.
🌊 9. Ocean Acidification
pH decreased 8.2→8.1 (30% more acidic); threatens coral reefs and shellfish.

Effects of Climate Change on Society

🌾 Food Security
Declining crop yields especially in Africa → hundreds of millions unable to produce or buy food.
💀 Human Mortality
Increased deaths from malnutrition and heat stress worldwide.
🌿 Ecosystems
Particularly vulnerable — many species face extinction unable to adapt fast enough.
🐟 Marine Ecosystems
Ocean acidification has major effects with adverse consequences on fish stocks.
🌊 Sea Level Rise
Melting ice sheets raise sea levels threatening coastal cities and island nations.
💧 Water Resources
Increased flood risks in wet season AND strongly reduced dry-season water supplies.
Philippine connection: Module includes image of residents on rooftops during Typhoon Ulysses hitting Luzon — connecting climate change to intensified Philippine typhoons.

Natural vs. Human-Enhanced Greenhouse Effect

NATURAL GREENHOUSE EFFECT

Atmospheric gases (CO₂, water vapor, methane) trap some solar energy → warms Earth's surface.

Without it: Earth = -18°C (currently +15°C). The natural greenhouse effect is essential for life.
HUMAN-ENHANCED GREENHOUSE EFFECT

Human activities dramatically increased GHG concentrations → more heat trapped → less escapes into space → abnormal warming.

This is the primary driver of current climate change.
